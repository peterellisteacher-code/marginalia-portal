SECTION 1 — THE TASK SHEET (authoritative)
========================================================

Year 11 Philosophy — Issues Study Independent Project

LEARNING INTENTIONS
- Identify philosophical issues and positions
- Understand the general structure of a philosophical argument
- Provide reasons to support philosophical issues and positions
- Differentiate good and bad arguments, using philosophical terminology
- Critically analyse assumptions, positions, and arguments
- Communicate philosophical issues and positions, with conventions observed

FORMAT
A written response of up to 800 words
OR a multimodal response of up to 5 minutes

Draft Due Date: Friday, Week 9, Term 2
Final Submission Date: Friday, Week 10, Term 2

TASK DESCRIPTION
An Issues Study is a study of a philosophical issue (meaning an unsolved
intellectual puzzle, not a social problem). Choose ONE philosophical issue
to study from the following:

METAPHYSICS
- Does the existence of evil pose a problem for a belief in the existence of God?
- Can the mind be separate from the body?

EPISTEMOLOGY
- Is there such a thing as truth, or is all truth relative?
- How can you tell the difference between what is real and what is imagined?

ETHICS
- What sort of ethical demands should we place on humans?
- To what extent do people have the right to serve only themselves?

You may also negotiate a different philosophical issue.

REQUIREMENTS
In your Issues Study you are required to:

1. Independently research, identify and explain MORE THAN ONE philosophical
   position on the issue.
2. Each philosophical position must offer its own unique perspective on the issue.
3. Critically analyse the strengths and weaknesses in each position.
4. Use evidence to justify your ideas.
5. Structure your argument so that the reader can clearly follow your flow of logic.
6. Communicate clearly, using philosophical terminology.
7. Reference sources appropriately, according to SACE referencing guidelines.

SECTION 2 — THE FOUR CRITERIA AND PERFORMANCE STANDARDS (A–E)
========================================================

The four criteria are:
  C1. Knowledge and Understanding
  C2. Reasoning
  C3. Critical Analysis
  C4. Communication

Performance Standards — plain-line version of the SACE table:

--- GRADE A ---
C1. Knowledge and Understanding:
    Clear and perceptive identification and understanding of philosophical
    issues, and of philosophical positions on issues. In-depth understanding
    of the general structure of a philosophical argument.

C2. Reasoning:
    Incisive reasoning, and use of relevant evidence, to support or contest
    philosophical issues and positions. Accurate differentiation between good
    and bad arguments.

C3. Critical Analysis:
    Consistently accurate and perceptive analysis of strengths and weaknesses
    of philosophical assumptions, positions, and arguments.

C4. Communication:
    Clear, coherent, and fluent communication of philosophical issues and
    positions, with conventions consistently observed. Accurate and consistent
    use of relevant philosophical terminology, with appropriate acknowledgment
    of sources.

--- GRADE B ---
C1. Knowledge and Understanding:
    Clear and thoughtful identification and understanding of philosophical
    issues, and of philosophical positions on issues. Well-informed
    understanding of the general structure of a philosophical argument.

C2. Reasoning:
    Well-considered reasoning, and use of mostly relevant evidence, to support
    or contest philosophical issues and positions. Mostly accurate
    differentiation between good and bad arguments.

C3. Critical Analysis:
    Mostly accurate and well-considered analysis of strengths and weaknesses
    of philosophical assumptions, positions, and arguments.

C4. Communication:
    Clear and coherent communication of philosophical issues and positions,
    with conventions mostly observed. Mostly accurate and appropriate use of
    philosophical terminology, with appropriate acknowledgment of sources.

--- GRADE C ---
C1. Knowledge and Understanding:
    Generally clear identification and understanding of philosophical issues,
    and of some philosophical positions on issues. Informed understanding of
    the general structure of a philosophical argument.

C2. Reasoning:
    Considered reasoning, and some use of evidence, to support or contest
    philosophical issues and positions. Generally accurate differentiation
    between good and bad arguments.

C3. Critical Analysis:
    Considered analysis of some strengths and weaknesses of philosophical
    assumptions, positions, and arguments.

C4. Communication:
    Competent communication of philosophical issues and positions, with some
    conventions observed. Generally appropriate use of philosophical
    terminology, with mostly appropriate acknowledgment of sources.

--- GRADE D ---
C1. Knowledge and Understanding:
    Some recognition of philosophical issues, and awareness of a philosophical
    position on an issue. Recognition of some of the general structure of a
    philosophical argument.

C2. Reasoning:
    Superficial or inconsistent reasoning, with some limited use of evidence,
    to support or contest a philosophical issue and/or position. Some accuracy
    in differentiation between good and bad arguments.

C3. Critical Analysis:
    Some identification of one or more strengths and/or weaknesses of a
    philosophical assumption, position, and/or argument.

C4. Communication:
    Partial communication of aspects of a philosophical issue and/or position.
    Use of a limited range of appropriate philosophical terminology, with some
    acknowledgment of sources.

--- GRADE E ---
C1. Knowledge and Understanding:
    Limited recognition of what is philosophical in an issue. Recognition of a
    structural feature of a philosophical argument.

C2. Reasoning:
    Attempted consideration of a philosophical issue or position. Emerging
    recognition of some differentiation between good and bad arguments.

C3. Critical Analysis:
    Identification of a strength or weakness of a philosophical assumption,
    position, or argument.

C4. Communication:
    Attempted communication of an aspect of a philosophical issue or position.
    Limited use of any philosophical terminology, with limited acknowledgment
    of sources.

NOTE ON THE MOST COMMON C-GRADE GAP:
The named gap at C is almost always the same: students state what a
philosopher believes but do not then analyse the strengths or weaknesses of
that view. They describe; they do not evaluate. To lift from C to B, a
student needs to ask — and answer — "What is right about this position? What
is wrong with it? Why might someone object?" for each philosopher they use.

SECTION 4 — A-GRADE EXEMPLAR (euthanasia / Virtue Ethics)

[Source: SACE Issues Study A-grade exemplar. ~800 words. Topic: Euthanasia
and Virtue Ethics.]

Euthanasia

Euthanasia is the act of painlessly putting to death persons suffering from
painful and/or incurable diseases or incapacitating physical disorders.
Literally the term euthanasia means 'a good death'. Euthanasia can be
classified as either active or passive. Active euthanasia involves the active
acceleration of a 'good' death by the use of drugs e.g. lethal injection,
administered by the persons themselves, a family member, a doctor or someone
else close to the patient. Most importantly the person does not die on their
own. Passive euthanasia involves no use of external aids or interjections
(like life support) but rather, allows death to occur naturally.

Euthanasia can also be classified as voluntary or involuntary. As the name
implies, voluntary euthanasia occurs when the patient asks to be killed.
Involuntary euthanasia occurs where a person is in a comatose state and thus
family members give consent. Moreover, euthanasia can also be classified as
'natural' or 'initiated'. In ethical terms, the former can be considered a
form of suicide, while the latter a form of murder.

Virtue Ethics

Virtue Ethics is a person-based moral theory first suggested by Aristotle,
in 'Nichomacheaen Ethics' approximately 350 BCE. Unlike utilitarianism —
whose principles focus on what is the right thing to do or the correct way
to act — the philosophies of Virtue Ethics focus on how one is to be a good
person. It teaches to define good people and to recognise the qualities/
virtues that make them 'good'.

In exploring Virtue Ethics, Aristotle believed that everything has a purpose
and man's purpose was happiness. Aristotle identified three forms of happiness:
  1. In living a life of enjoyment and pleasure
  2. In being a free member of society
  3. In being a philosopher

The happiness that Aristotle talked about was that of the community, not just
the individual, since he saw people not only as rational beings but social
beings. Therefore the well being of a group was more important than that of a
single member. Aristotle believed that virtues were to be found in compromise,
located in the balance between the vices of excess and deficiency, e.g.
courage being found between cowardice and foolhardiness. Aristotle stated
that every person should work to develop his or her own character by making a
habit out of virtue. This will automatically lead to good actions, as a good
person will always make good decisions. He believed that future generations
could then be taught goodness from their seniors as virtue should be taught
by example, not through a set of rules.

Euthanasia and Virtue Ethics

As medical technology has advanced, the ability to extend the lives of
patients suffering from various medical conditions has increased. The ethical
question arises of whether the lives of patients should be extended when they
may be helpless or suffering. The following focuses on the issue of active
and voluntary euthanasia in relation to the application of Virtue Ethics.

It would not be presumptuous to assume that people suffering from great
illness and pain would not be living the eudaimon life. If there were a way
to improve their physical well being, Aristotle's Virtue Ethics would support
this. A patient contemplating euthanasia could consider the implications of
Aristotle's virtue of courage — the 'Golden Mean' between cowardice and
foolhardiness. The 'right' course of action could however be open to
interpretation. You could claim that euthanasia would be the courageous act
by facing death and having the strength to free loved ones from the emotional,
heartbreaking burden of looking after a terminally ill or pain-wracked
relative. On the other hand, euthanasia could be seen as an act of cowardice,
of lacking courage and endurance and violating the sanctity of life. It could
be perceived as a rash action taken in a moment of weakness or despondency.

Aristotle was concerned with the good of society as a whole, above the
individual. In societies where resources are scarce, euthanasia may well make
a significant difference to the well being of the society as a whole.
Extending the lives of terminally ill patients requires huge amounts of money
and the time, effort and emotional support of family members. Under these
circumstances, it may be viewed as a courageous and noble act for someone to
take their own life when very ill. Even in our society where people are living
longer and surviving near fatal accidents there may be huge financial benefits
to society if those who are unproductive are euthanised. This view however,
doesn't seem in keeping with Aristotle's virtues of patience and temperance.

The application of Virtue Ethics as put forward by Aristotle appears too
vague and therefore difficult in sensitive and complex issues such as
euthanasia. Perhaps Aristotle would conclude that a person achieving
Eudaimonia would have the judgement and wisdom to make the 'right decision'.

SECTION 6 — MODEL ESSAY (freedom of speech / Mill + Nietzsche)
========================================================

[Source: Milly CJ — high-quality student essay included as a further
exemplar. Topic: Should freedom of speech be allowed regardless of people
or place? Philosophers used: John Stuart Mill (On Liberty) and Nietzsche
(will to power).]

Should freedom of speech be allowed regardless of people or place?

Today more than ever, freedom of speech and the complex ethics surrounding it
are hot topics. With right-wing politicians such as Trump and Hanson on the
rise, our ideas around the politically correct are being challenged and
raising questions. If we are only allowed to have appropriate, non-offensive
opinions, is it truly freedom of speech? Should there be certain situations
where freedom of speech is not allowed, or does that defeat its purpose? In
this essay, I will be exploring the idea that freedom of speech should be
allowed regardless of people or place.

As John Mill, author of the influential 1858 book 'On Liberty', explains,
there is a difference between causing offence and causing harm, and the
latter should be avoided. According to Mill, opposition and rebellion should
be allowed, as in this way we can challenge, update, or solidify our own
views. However, if this escalates and becomes incitement for harm, it is
unacceptable. For example, protesting about animal rights in a public place
may make certain people feel uncomfortable but is still tolerated, while an
angry mob protesting outside a meat factory which could potentially turn
violent would not be. Personally, I think this is a sound foundation for
freedom of speech. However, it could potentially lead to issues and disconnect
if one group/individual feels something is simply causing offence, while the
other believe it's causing harm. A leader might think they're only stating an
offensive truth when saying women shouldn't dress promiscuously, but women
who do could be street harassed as a result of this. Regardless, from Mill's
perspective, it shouldn't matter about people or place, only whether it will
cause harm or not.

However, even though in some cases freedom of speech will not cause harm, it
can still be greatly offensive and deeply hurtful. Although homophobic
campaigners at a homosexual person's funeral may not cause tangible harm, it
would still be extremely upsetting and traumatic. In my opinion, there are
some situations where it is inhumane, disrespectful and insensitive to use
freedom of speech, despite not causing harm. The only issue is, how do we
decide which places are considered appropriate and which are not? It would be
easy to simply cite 'common sense', but we are all diverse people with our
own set of morals, values and versions of the truth, which makes it far from
easy. As well as this, is it fair that the person/group attacked by freedom
of speech are given priority over the person/group who believe they are
defending their rights? For example, members of a Russian political punk band
were jailed for 2 years after singing profanity-laced insults directed at
Putin in St. Peter's cathedral, a sacred icon for religious Russians. In this
situation, the rights of the deeply offended Catholics were favoured over
those of the Russian women, and they were severely punished for their
enthusiastic freedom of speech. Essentially, this is saying that the women
have fewer rights and are lesser beings than the Catholics, which seems
extremely unfair and unjust. All humans have equal rights, and this should be
upheld on both ends, irrespective of freedom of speech.

From a completely different standpoint, we can also analyse Nietzsche's will
to power in the context of freedom of speech. In this case, freedom of speech
would undisputedly be allowed no matter what, as this would bring power to
the individual and allow them to exert their powers over others. Nietzsche
was passionate about avoiding the herd and anti-conformism, and so
restriction of rights such as freedom of speech would seem a grave insult and
threat to identity. To me, however, this way of thinking is very selfish and
cruel, as it doesn't consider anyone other than the individual. Unfortunately
for Nietzsche, by nature we are herd animals who crave positive social
interactions. His line of thinking cannot realistically be applied to freedom
of speech, unless we throw away all empathy and understanding for others,
something that would lead to a very soulless society.

To conclude, it's my belief that complete freedom of speech regardless of
people or place is too dangerous to exist in our society, and so it would be
wise to follow Mill's philosophy over Nietzsche's. Although freedom of speech
can be used for positive things like fighting oppression, making art, and
raising awareness, the problem is that it can just as easily be used for hate
speeches and to cause harm — something we have sadly seen with the recent
rise of racism towards non-Caucasians, even here in our own country. Freedom
of speech shouldn't be something discriminatory bigots use as a shield to
hide behind, but unfortunately this seems to be the case. For now, it appears
as though we must continue to validate freedom of speech only as individuals
rather than as a whole, since with so much discrimination, hatred and
division in our world, we have not yet succeeded in achieving universal
unanimity.
