Existentialism

Jean-Paul Sartre

Republic of Silence

Sartre's wartime essay on radical freedom — the claim that even under occupation, every act remains a choice and an exercise of authenticity. Short, intense.

Sartre

Sartre — overview

A class-prepared overview of Sartre's central concepts: existence precedes essence, bad faith, the look of the Other, anguish.

Simone de Beauvoir

de Beauvoir — situation and freedom

How "situation" conditions but does not abolish freedom — Beauvoir's extension of Sartrean existentialism to gendered embodiment. The ethical core of The Second Sex.

Albert Camus

Camus — the absurd and revolt

The absurd is the collision between human longing for meaning and the silence of the universe. Camus's response: revolt, freedom, passion.

Existentialism — four thinkers

A comparative overview of Sartre, de Beauvoir, Camus, and a fourth (Kierkegaard or Heidegger, depending on edition). Useful for orientation before reading the primary texts.

Virtue, attention, and compassion

Iris Murdoch

The Sovereignty of Good

Murdoch's defining argument: morality is not primarily about what we do but how we see. Loving attention to particular people is the moral act. A counterweight to consequentialism.

Discuss in chamber → ✓ cached
Martha Nussbaum

Compassion and Reason

Emotions, Nussbaum argues, are forms of cognition — not noise on top of reasoning. Compassion in particular is a structured judgement about another's suffering and our shared vulnerability.

Discuss in chamber → ✓ cached

Religion and morality

Jonathan Haidt

Haidt on Religion

Available in three depths (bare minimum, standard, more than minimum). Haidt's argument: religion's primary function is moral binding, not metaphysical claim — and secular morality lacks something the binding does.

Discuss in chamber → ✓ cached

Aesthetics — what is art and what does it mean?

Wimsatt & Beardsley

The

The argument that what an author intended is neither available nor desirable as a standard for interpretation. The poem is a public object; meaning lives in the work, not the maker.

Discuss in chamber → ✓ cached
companion essay

The Intentional and the Unintentional

A counterweight or extension to Wimsatt & Beardsley — what role does intention play in interpretation, if any?

Mind, consciousness, and simulation

overview

Identity Theory and Functionalism

Two contrasting accounts of what mental states are. Identity theory: mental states ARE brain states. Functionalism: mental states are defined by their causal roles, not their physical substrate.

Discuss in chamber → ✓ cached
overview

Problems for Identity Theory

The standard objections — multiple realisability, Nagel's "what is it like to be a bat", the conceivability of philosophical zombies.

Discuss in chamber → ✓ cached
overview

Dualism

The view that minds and brains are distinct kinds of thing. Substance vs property dualism. Why dualism survives the rise of neuroscience — and where it doesn't.

Discuss in chamber → ✓ cached
overview

Simulation Theory

Bostrom's argument that we are likely living in a simulation, the standard responses, and the philosophical question of whether the answer would matter even if we knew it.

overview

What is a Human Being?

A class lecture on what makes us distinctively human — biological, rational, social, narrative — and what each criterion includes and excludes.

Applied ethics — personhood, abortion, animals

Peter Singer

Singer on Abortion

Singer's utilitarian argument: personhood depends on capacities (sentience, self-awareness, future preference), not species membership. Implications for abortion, infanticide, and animal ethics.

Discuss in chamber → ✓ cached
Don Marquis

Why Abortion is Immoral

The Future-Like-Ours argument. Killing is wrong because it deprives the victim of a future of value — and a fetus has such a future. A non-religious argument against abortion.

Discuss in chamber → ✓ cached
Judith Jarvis Thomson

A Defence of Abortion

The famous violinist thought experiment. Even granting that a fetus is a person, the right to life does not automatically entail the right to use another person's body.

Discuss in chamber → ✓ cached
