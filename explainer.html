What is an ?

Principle One

You choose the question.

Every other piece of assessment in this course gives you the question. The Issues Study does not. Your first task is to negotiate a question with your teacher — a real philosophical question that you genuinely want to think about, drawn from ethics, , or .

This is harder than it sounds. A topic ("free will") is not a question. "Are we genuinely free, or is every choice the product of prior causes?" is a question. The difference matters: a topic invites you to summarise what others have said. A question forces you to take a position.

Principle Two

You take more than one position seriously.

An A-grade Issues Study identifies multiple philosophical positions on the question and explains why people hold each one — including positions you don't share. A C-grade response usually engages with one perspective and treats it as obvious.

The reason is structural. You can't critically evaluate a position you haven't first reconstructed sympathetically. every view before you criticise it.

Principle Three

You evaluate before you defend.

Critical Analysis (CA1) means analysing the strengths and weaknesses of the positions and arguments you've described. This is the section students most often skip. It is the section that most directly distinguishes A from C.

Ask of every position: What is its strongest point? What is its most damaging weakness? What objection has it not yet answered?

Principle Four

You defend your own position with reasoning, not opinion.

"I personally feel" is not an argument. The final move in the Issues Study is to take a position of your own — and to defend it with logical reasoning anchored to the readings you've engaged with. Your view should follow from your analysis, not stand apart from it.

Principle Five

800 words. Or 5 minutes. Or .

You can submit this as a written essay (max 800 words), a spoken oral (max 5 minutes), or an equivalent multimodal piece (video, podcast, illustrated essay). All four design criteria — Knowledge & Understanding, Reasoning, Critical Analysis, Communication — must be addressed regardless of mode.

A and C, side by side

Below is an extract from an A-grade and a C-grade exemplar response, both on the same question about personal identity. The marginalia (in red) point to the moves the A response makes that the C response does not.

A response

Locke argues that personal identity over time is constituted by psychological continuity — particularly the continuity of memory. However, this faces a serious objection from Reid: if I cannot remember what I did at age four, am I no longer the same person who did it? One reply, due to Parfit, is that what matters is overlapping chains of memory, not direct recall. I find Parfit's reply more defensible because it accommodates ordinary cases of forgetting without abandoning the psychological criterion entirely.

C response

Locke says that personal identity is about memory. If you can remember being someone, you are that person. This is interesting because it suggests that someone with amnesia might not be the same person they were before. I think Locke makes a good point because memory is important to who we are.

Notice the moves: the A response names philosophers, presents an objection, presents a reply, and then takes a position with reasons. The C response describes one view, agrees with it, and stops.

The four design criteria

Every Issues Study is graded against these four criteria. The site marks which sections build evidence for which criteria; you'll see the codes (KU / R / CA / C) appear throughout.

  • KU Knowledge and Understanding — that you have identified the philosophical issue and the positions on it.
  • R Reasoning — that your arguments are logical, well-supported by evidence, and that you can distinguish good arguments from bad.
  • CA Critical Analysis — that you have examined the strengths and weaknesses of the positions, not just described them.
  • C Communication — that you have expressed yourself clearly, used philosophical terminology accurately, and acknowledged your sources.

Notes in the margin

A note from the editor Don't try to write the essay before you've found the question. The negotiation step is the assessment, not a preliminary to it.
Common pitfall Choosing a topic that's been "settled" — abortion, free will, simulation — doesn't mean the question is settled. Choose your question carefully within the topic.
From SACE moderators The single most impactful thing you can do is phrase your topic as a question. Not "Free will" — but "If neuroscience can predict our choices before we make them, do we still have free will?"
A note about the AI The Socratic chamber will never write for you. It will ask you questions designed to sharpen yours. That's the point.
