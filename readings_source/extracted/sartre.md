Jean-Paul Sartre
FUNDAMENTAL IDEAS
Existence Precedes Essence:
•
Concept: Like de Beauvoir, one of the most fundamental tenets of
Sartre's existentialism is the idea that "existence precedes essence." In
simple terms, it means that individuals first exist, encounter
themselves, and then define their essence or nature through their
actions and choices.
•
Implication: This rejects the notion of a predetermined human nature
or essence. There's no fixed "human nature" since there's no God or
external entity to define such a nature. Thus, individuals are
responsible for creating their own essence through their choices and
actions.
Radical Freedom:
•
Concept: Sartre believed that humans are condemned to be free.
There's no external force or destiny determining our actions. Every
choice we make defines our essence.
•
Implication: This freedom is both liberating and burdensome. While it
allows individuals to shape their destinies, it also brings the weight of
responsibility for every action and its consequences.
Bad Faith (Mauvaise foi):
•
Concept: Sartre introduced the notion of "bad faith" as a form of selfdeception. It's the act of denying one's complete freedom and
responsibility by attributing one's actions to external causes or societal
norms.
•
Implication: Living in bad faith is living inauthentically. It's a way to
escape the anxiety of freedom by conforming to societal expectations
or attributing decisions to factors beyond one's control.
Anguish and Absurdity:
•
Concept: Recognisng one's complete freedom and the absence of any
predetermined essence or moral framework leads to feelings of
anguish and absurdity.
•
Implication: The world lacks inherent meaning, and the realisation of
this can lead to a sense of despair. However, it also opens the door for
individuals to create their own meanings and values.

The Look and the Other:
•
Concept: In his work "Being and Nothingness," Sartre explores the
idea of "the Look." It's the awareness that others can perceive us,
leading to a feeling of being objectified or defined by their perceptions.
•
Implication: This introduces the idea that our self-awareness and
identity are shaped not only by our internal perceptions but also by our
interactions and relationships with others.
Commitment and Action:
•
Concept: For Sartre, existentialism isn't just a philosophical
contemplation but a call to action. Individuals should be committed to
their choices and take active roles in shaping their destinies and the
world around them.
•
Implication: Authentic existence involves not just recognising one's
freedom but actively engaging with the world, making choices, and
taking responsibility for their outcomes.
Existential Marxism:
•
Concept: Later in his life, Sartre attempted to reconcile existentialism
with Marxism, emphasising the role of societal structures in shaping
individual experiences while maintaining the core existentialist tenets of
freedom and responsibility.
•
Implication: This synthesis aimed to combine the individual freedom
emphasised by existentialism with the societal critique offered by
Marxism.
In essence, Jean-Paul Sartre's existentialism delves deeply into the human
condition, exploring themes of freedom, responsibility, authenticity, and interpersonal
relationships. It offers a profound reflection on the nature of existence, the
challenges of freedom, and the quest for authentic living in a world devoid of inherent
meaning.
Jean-Paul Sartre's Biography and Context:
Sartre's existentialism was deeply rooted in his life's events and the thinkers he
engaged with.
Early Life:
Born in Paris in 1905, Sartre lost his father at a young age and was raised by his
mother and maternal grandfather. His early years were marked by a passion for
literature and philosophy.

Academic Pursuits:
Sartre studied at the École Normale Supérieure in Paris, where he formed significant
friendships, most notably with Simone de Beauvoir. He was influenced by German
phenomenology, especially the works of Edmund Husserl and Martin Heidegger.
World War II and Resistance:
Sartre served as a soldier during World War II and was captured by German forces
in 1940. He spent around a year as a prisoner of war before being released. After his
return to Paris, he became an active member of the French Resistance.
His wartime experiences, witnessing human resilience, freedom, and despair amidst
oppression, deeply influenced his existentialist writings. The themes of freedom,
responsibility, and action became even more pronounced in his work.
Post-War Paris:
After the war, Sartre became a central figure in the Parisian intellectual scene. He
founded the influential journal "Les Temps Modernes" and engaged in various
political and social issues.
His post-war writings and activism reflect a commitment to existentialist principles
applied to real-world issues, emphasising individual freedom and societal
responsibility.
Engagement with Other Thinkers:
Sartre's existentialism was in dialogue with other existential thinkers like Albert
Camus, Friedrich Nietzsche, and Fyodor Dostoevsky. However, he had
disagreements, most notably with Camus over issues like revolutionary violence.
These engagements enriched his thought, leading to both convergences and
divergences with other existentialist thinkers.
Jean-Paul Sartre's Existential Philosophy:
1. Existence Precedes Essence:
Sartre's foundational claim is that existence comes before essence for humans.
Unlike inanimate objects, humans are not born with a predetermined purpose or
nature. Instead, they forge their essence through their choices and actions in the
world.
Extended: In a world devoid of divine determination, humans are thrown into
existence with the freedom to define themselves. This freedom is both a blessing
and a curse: it allows for self-definition but also carries the weight of responsibility.

What we Ought to do: Sartre believed individuals ought to recognise and embrace
their radical freedom. They should not hide behind societal norms or preconceived
roles but instead actively define their essence through authentic choices.
2. Radical Freedom and Responsibility:
Sartre posited that humans possess an immense degree of freedom. Even in
situations of external constraint, individuals retain the freedom of choice in how they
respond or interpret their circumstances.
Extended: This freedom, however, is accompanied by a profound sense of
responsibility. Every choice made contributes to defining one's essence and, in a
broader sense, contributes to defining what it means to be human.
What we Ought to do: Individuals ought to be acutely aware of their freedom and
the corresponding responsibility. They should act authentically, understanding that
each action has consequences not just for oneself but for the collective definition of
humanity.
3. Bad Faith (Mauvaise foi):
Bad faith is the act of self-deception, where individuals deny their freedom and
responsibility, often to avoid the anxiety associated with them.
Extended: People in bad faith might attribute their actions to societal norms, their
role in society, or external circumstances. By doing so, they escape the existential
anguish of freedom but live inauthentically.
What we Ought to do: Sartre argued that individuals ought to confront and
overcome bad faith. They should recognise and accept their freedom and the
inherent responsibility, striving for authenticity even when it's uncomfortable.
4. Anguish and Absurdity:
Recognising one's freedom and the lack of inherent meaning or moral structure in
the world can lead to feelings of anguish and a sense of absurdity.
Extended: The absurdity stems from the realisation that the world lacks inherent
purpose, yet humans continuously seek meaning. Anguish is the emotional response
to the weight of freedom and the responsibility it entails.
What we Ought to do: While these feelings are natural, Sartre believed individuals
ought to confront them head-on. Instead of succumbing to despair, they should
create their own meaning and values, forging purpose in an indifferent universe.
5. The Look and the Other:

Sartre explored interpersonal dynamics, emphasising how individuals become
objectified in the gaze of the other. This realisation that one is being perceived can
lead to feelings of objectification and alienation.
Extended: This dynamic highlights the tension between one's subjective experience
and the external perception of oneself. It underscores the complexities of human
relationships and the challenge of maintaining authenticity in interpersonal
interactions.
What we Ought to do: Sartre suggested that individuals ought to be aware of this
dynamic, striving for genuine relationships based on mutual recognition and respect,
rather than reducing the other to mere objects.
6. Commitment and Action:
Beyond abstract contemplation, Sartre's existentialism is a call to committed action in
the world.
Extended: In a universe devoid of preordained values, individuals have the
responsibility to create values through their actions. These actions not only define
the individual but also shape the world.
What we Ought to do: Sartre believed individuals ought to be actively engaged in
the world. Authentic existence involves not only recognising one's freedom and
responsibility but also acting on them, committedly shaping one's destiny and
contributing to societal change.
At its core, Sartre's existentialism is a call to action—a call for individuals to
recognise their radical freedom, confront the inherent challenges of existence, and
actively shape their destinies in an ever-complex world.
