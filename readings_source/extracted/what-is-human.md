[Slide 10]
The Genetic Criterion
7/1/20XX
Pitch deck title
10
P1: Only those granted personhood are entitled to moral and legal rights.
P2: Personhood is reserved for humans.
P3: Every human has human DNA, and only humans have human DNA.
Conclusion: So long as you have human DNA you are entitled to all the moral and legal rights of personhood.
The ‘Genetic Criterion’ is famously attributed to Judge John Noonan (1926-2017)
Noonan served as a federal judge in the United States for thirty years.
Known for his philosophy of personhood.
Notably, his views are shaped by his Catholic faith and his education in law and philosophy.
His criterion was primarily designed to think about Abortion law.

[Slide 11]
Cognitive Criteria
Mary Anne Warren (1946-2010)
American philosopher who specialised in personhood.
Like Noonan, Warren was primarily concerned with the abortion debate.
She was pro-choice and opposed to the Genetic Criterion.
She identified five criteria that about personhood:
7/1/20XX
Pitch deck title
11
Consciousness – Defined as the capacity to experience emotions and sensations.
Reason – Defined as the ability to process complex problems.
Self-Motivated Activity – Deciding to act based on a conscious decision.
Communication – The ability to communicate ‘by whatever means’ on a variety of topics.
Self-Awareness – Recognising oneself as an individual and/or member of a group.

[Slide 12]
Cognitive Criteria
7/1/20XX
Pitch deck title
12
Not all of Warren’s criteria are required to qualify for personhood.
1, 2, and 3 might be sufficient (consciousness, reasoning, self-motivated activity).
For example, we might lose the capacity to communicate, but we would still retain personhood.
Mary Anne Warren (1946-2010)

[Slide 13]
Cognitive Criteria
7/1/20XX
Pitch deck title
13
"We needn't suppose that an entity must have all of these attributes to be properly considered a person; (1) and (2) alone may well be sufficient for personhood, and quite probable (1) - (3) are sufficient. Neither do we need to insist that any one of these criteria is necessary for personhood, although once again (1) and (2) look like fairly good candidates for necessary conditions, as does (3), if 'actively' is construed so as to include the activity of reasoning."
Mary Anne Warren (1946-2010)

Cognitive Criteria

Warren qualifies that her definition is “species neutral”, writing:
“…it leaves room for the logical possibility that there can be persons that are not human beings, or human beings that are not persons. What makes a being a person, on this definition, is not its biological humanity, but its intelligence, and its capacity for thought, reason, reflection and self-awareness.”
Mary Anne Warren (1946-2010)

Sentience Theory
Peter Singer (1946-)
7/1/20XX
Pitch deck title
16
Personhood relies on sentience: the ability to feel pleasure and pain
We cannot exclude non-humans from moral consideration, because they can suffer like humans
Acknowledges that differences between individual members of different species may be morally relevant in determining how we treat them
But we cannot simply mistreat animals because they do not have human membership

[Slide 17]
Gradient Theory
American Zoologist Charles Manning Child (1869-1954)
7/1/20XX
Pitch deck title
17
Personhood comes in degrees - you can have more or less personhood E.g. the development of a foetus
Can be gradually lost or gradually gained
Some believe that personhood is forfeited when you greatly violate the laws of society through Inhumane actions (this is an argument for the death penalty)
